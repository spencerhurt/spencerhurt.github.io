About

I am an undergraduate at CU Boulder studying astrophysics and planning to graduate in the spring of 2022. Afterward, I will work towards my Ph.D. in astrophysics and hope to pursue a career in academica. My current research interests revolve around the detection and characterization of exoplanet systems. Using these observations, I hope to uncover the processes of planet formation and investigate the prospects for life beyond Earth. While at Boulder, I am working with Dr. Meredith MacGregor at the Center for Astrophysics and Space Astronomy, modeling ALMA images of eccentric circumstellar debris disks. With these models, we can place constraints on the presence of planets sheparding material into non-circular orbits and explore the dynamical history of a system.

Having grown up in western Colorado, I like to spend my spare time hiking, biking, and anything else outdoors! I am also an amateur astrophotographer, enjoy growing trees and other plants, and play the violin.

Research

My long-term research interests are focused on using new instrumentation and novel data analysis techniques to overcome observational biases and fill gaps in the known exoplanet and disk populations. This will allow us to better understand the processes of planet formation and evolution while searching for potentially habitable worlds.

This ADS library contains a (very young) list of my publications.

Modeling Circumstellar Disks

I am currently working with Dr. Meredith MacGregor to model ALMA images of circumstellar debris disks, which are composed by the remnants of planet formation. Using these observations, we can conduct "planetary archaeology" and explore the planet-forming and dynamical history of a system. I am particularly interested in eccentric structures, which often indicate the presence of sub-stellar companions sheperding material into non-circular orbits.

Detecting Sub-Stellar Objects

My first research position was with Dr. Sam Quinn and Dr. David Latham at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics, where I worked with the TESS Follow-up Observing Program to identify exoplanet candidates. I eventually began an independent project analyzing a decade of radial velocities from the bright, nearby star Vega, identifying a planet candidate and placing new detection limits for orbital companions. The resulting first-author paper was published in The Astronomical Journal and has been featured throughout the media. Feel free to check out CU Boulder's press release.

In 2019, I began to contribute to RadVel, a Python package used to search for and model exoplanets in radial-velocity time series. I am currently collaborating with Dr. BJ Fulton to apply this work to data from Gliese 411, the fourth closest stellar system to Earth. In a first-author paper currently in preparation, I investigate several disputed planetary signals and perform a detailed characterization of the system.

This summer, I will be conducting research with Dr. Mike Liu through the University of Hawaii Institute for Astronomy REU Program. My work will focus on using wide-field surveys to detect brown dwarfs and exoplanets at large orbital separations from their host stars. By searching older stars for companions, we hope to test theoretical substellar models at old ages, a parameter space that has largely gone unexplored.

Characterizing Planets

While exoplanet characterization has traditionally focused on their atmospheres, I am interested in determining the internal structure and large-scale geology of these objects. Lately, I have been testing different approaches towards measuring the rotationally-induced oblateness of a planet. This parameter can help reveal an object's more nuanced orbital motion and formation history.

Investigating Stellar Activity and Variability

Using our spectra and TESS data from Vega, I found that the star's surface is likely dominated by plages rather than dark spots. Additionally, components of Vega's rotational modulation appear to evolve over long timescales, indicating a mechanism such as a failed fossil field may be responsible for the A-type star's unexpected magnetism. Investigating this chromospheric activity helps us better understand stellar physics and develop techniques to disentangle planets and rotational modulation in radial velocity data.

While working with Dr. MacGregor, I also use ALMA data to search for stellar flares in sub-millimeter wavelengths. Understanding the flaring mechanisms of different stars gives insight towards their habitability.

Outreach

As a first-generation scientist coming from a low-income family, finding a sense of belonging in STEM has often been a challenge. This is only exacerbated as a member of the queer community and as someone with several disabilities. Through outreach efforts, I hope to join my peers and build a more welcoming community in astrophysics while helping others find their passion for science.

I regularly volunteer with the Grand Mesa Observatory, located near my hometown of Grand Junction, giving talks or contributing to stargazing events. I also help maintain equipment and contributed to a scientific observing facility focused on exoplanet follow-up. This last summer, I designed an observing and data analysis curriculum to introduce local high school students to astrophysics.

Since arriving at CU Boulder, I have regularly joined graduate students at the Sommers-Bausch Observatory to deliver virtual open house nights to the public. This involves operating equipment while explaining observational techniques and the science behind different astrophysical objects.
