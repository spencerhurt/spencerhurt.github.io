About

I am an undergraduate at CU Boulder studying astrophysics and planning to graduate in the spring of 2022. Afterward, I will work towards my Ph.D. in astrophysics and hope to pursue a life-long research career. My current interests revolve around the detection and characterization of exoplanets, along with their implications for life beyond Earth. While at Boulder, I am working with Dr. Meredith MacGregor at the Center for Astrophysics and Space Astronomy, modeling circumstellar debris disks and exploring their implications for planet formation and evolution.

As a western Colorado native, I like to spend my spare time hiking, biking, and anything else outdoors! I am also an amateur astrophotographer, enjoy growing trees and other plants, and play the violin.

Research

My long-term research interests are focused on overcoming observational biases and filling gaps in the known exoplanet and disk populations. This will allow us to better understand the processes of planet formation and evolution while exploring the prospects for life beyond our Solar System.

This ADS library contains a (very young) list of my publications.

Debris Disks and Stellar Variability

While at CU Boulder, I am working with Dr. Meredith MacGregor to model ALMA images of circumstellar debris disks, which allow us to uncover the dynamical history of planetary systems. Additionally, I have been searching sub-millimeter data for stellar variability, which can give hints towards the habitability of a system.

Detecting Exoplanet Systems

My first-ever research position was with Dr. Sam Quinn and Dr. David Latham at the Harvard-Smithsonian CfA, where I worked with the TESS Follow-up Observing Program to identify exoplanet candidates. I eventually began an independent project analyzing a decade of radial velocities of the bright star Vega, which led to a planet candidate, new detection limits for orbital companions, and an unexpected excursion into stellar activity and magnetism. The resulting first-author paper can be found here on arXiv.

I have also collaborated with Dr. BJ Fulton through the California Planet Search, maintaining and adding functionality to RadVel, a Python package used to search for and model exoplanets in radial velocity time series. I have been applying this work to data from Gliese 411, the fourth closest stellar system to Earth, and am anticipating a first-author paper from this project.

Characterizing Planets

While exoplanet characterization has traditionally focused on their atmospheres, I am interested in determining the internal structure and large-scale geology of these objects. Lately, I have been modeling different approaches towards measuring the rotationally-induced oblateness of a planet. This parameter can help reveal the more nuanced orbital motion and formation history of a planet.

About

As a first-generation scientist coming from a low-income family, finding a sense of belonging in STEM has often been a challenge. This is only exacerbated as a member of the queer community and as someone with several disabilities. Through outreach efforts, I hope to join my peers and build a more welcoming community in astrophysics while helping others find their passion for science.

I regularly volunteer with the Grand Mesa Observatory, located near my hometown of Grand Junction, giving talks or contributing to stargazing events. I also help maintain equipment and contributed to a scientific observing facility focused on exoplanet follow-up. This last summer, I designed an observing and data analysis curriculum to introduce local high school students to astrophysics.

Since arriving at CU Boulder, I have regularly joined graduate students at the Sommers-Bausch Observatory to deliver virtual open house nights to the public. This involves operating equipment while explaining observational techniques and the science behind different astrophysical objects.
