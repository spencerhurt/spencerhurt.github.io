About

I am an NSF Graduate Research Fellow in the Department of Earth Sciences at the University of Oregon (located on the traditional homeland of the Kalapuya people). Here, I work with Dr. Josef Dufek to study the physics of volcanic eruptions. I am particularly interested in the chemical and microphysical processes that control the transport of sulfur compounds through volcanic plumes and the implications for subsequent climate forcing. In May of 2022, I graduated summa cum laude from CU Boulder, where I studied astrophysics and planetary science as a Goldwater and Astronaut Scholar. During this time, my research focused on the detection and characterization of exoplanet systems.

Research

My research combines computational modeling with fieldwork and laboratory experiments to understand the chemical and microphysical processes that control the formation and transport of sulfur compounds in volcanic plumes. When eruptions deliver large amounts of sulfate aerosols to the stratosphere, they often remain suspended for long timescales and reflect sunlight, causing the Earth's surface to cool. This climate forcing can be both dramatic and sudden; in extreme cases, it may be catastrophic on a global scale. Understanding what conditions enable the transport of sulfate aerosols is critical when preparing for and responding to these wide-spread, long-term hazards. My Google Scholar and ORCID pages contain a list of my publications and below you can find descriptions of my past research on the detection and characterization of exoplanet systems.

Modeling Circumstellar Debris Disks

While at CU Boulder, I worked with Dr. Meredith MacGregor to model high-resolution images of circumstellar debris disks taken by the Atacama Large Millimeter/sub-millimeter Array (ALMA). These disks form from colliding planetesimals—the remnants of planet formation—and provide valuable information about the dynamical evolution of a planetary system. I led the modeling of HD 53143, which is currently the most eccentric (non-circular) debris disk known to dateThe resulting paper was featured in a press conference at the 240th meeting of the American Astronomical Society (AAS) and a press release by the National Radio Astronomy Observatory (NRAO).. Additionally, my senior thesis measured the alignment between debris disks and their host stars, helping understand which mechanisms misalign planetary orbits with their star's rotation axes. This work was recognized by the Department of Astrophysical and Planetary Sciences with the Theodore Snow Scholarship, awarded to the graduating student with the best honors thesis.

Detecting Exoplanets

I collaborated with Dr. David Latham and Dr. Samuel Quinn at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics and Dr. Benjamin Fulton at Caltech to search for low-mass planets around nearby stars using the radial-velocity method. This resulted in two papers identifying candidate planets around Vega CU Boulder and the CfA issued press releases about this candidate exoplanet, which was featured on Forbes, Space.com, and many other news sites. If confirmed, it would be one of the hottest exoplanets known today!, one of the brightest stars in our night sky, and Gliese 411, the fourth closest stellar system to Earth. During this time, I also maintained and added functionality to RadVel, a popular Python package used to model radial-velocity time series and search for exoplanets.

Characterizing Sub-Stellar Objects

I spent several summers working with Dr. Michael Liu at the University of Hawaii Institute for Astronomy, fitting model atmospheres to the largest sample of benchmark ultracool dwarfs to date. In a paper currently in preparation, we use these results to characterize systematic behavior in the model atmospheres commonly used to study these objectsFun fact: many of the same microphysical processes used to model dust and cloud formation in the atmospheres of brown dwarfs and giant exoplanets apply to the formation and transport of sulfuric aerosols in volcanic plumes!.

Outreach

I previously volunteered with the Grand Mesa Observatory, located near my hometown in western Colorado. Here, I helped organize stargazing events and public talks while maintaining and operating equipment. I also organized an observational astronomy and data analysis curriculum to give local high school students a hands-on introduction to scientific research and computer programming. While at CU Boulder, I also regularly ran virtual open-house nights at the Sommers-Bausch Observatory.

As my research transitions to volcanology and the Earth sciences, I hope to focus on additional outreach and service. I am particularly interested in efforts to decolonize the geosciences and making field work and research accessible to people with disabilities.
