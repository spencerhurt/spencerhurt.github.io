About

I am an engineer for the Wyoming Infrared Observatory (WIRO), home to a 2.3-meter telescope located at the top of Jelm Mountain in southern Wyoming. Here, I am responsible for maintenance and upgrades to the facility, specializing in electronics and software development. Most recently, I have been replacing our telescope control system after a series of lightning strikes took out critical components of the earlier system.

Previously, I was an NSF Graduate Research Fellow at the University of Oregon Department of Earth Sciences, where I studied the chemical and microphysical processes that control the transport of sulfur compounds through volcanic plumes and the implications for climate forcing. While I enjoyed this research and graduate school, personal circumstances required I return close to home before completing my degree, leading me to Laramie. Before Oregon, I graduated summa cum laude from CU Boulder, where I studied astrophysics and planetary science as a Goldwater and Astronaut Scholar. During this time, my research focused on the detection and characterization of exoplanet systems.

Besides work, I am an avid birder and amateur wildlife photographer; some of the things I see (including low-quality photographs) are shared on iNaturalist and eBird. I have been working to formalize my knowledge of our natural world and recently graduated from the Wyoming Naturalist Program. Some additional interests include backpacking, biking, astrophotography, and gardening.

Research

My current research interests focus on wetlands and riparian zones, particularly in harsh environments such as the desert or Arctic. Building on my background in physics, Earth science, modeling, and data science, as well as my experience conducting fieldwork for the University of Wyoming Biodiversity Institute, I hope to contribute to a holistic understanding of how climate, geology, flora, fauna, and humans both influence and depend on our rivers and streams.

My My Google Scholar and NASA ADS pages contain a list of my publications. Below, you can find descriptions of my past research in volcanology and astronomy.

Modeling Volcanic Plumes

At the University of Oregon, I collaborated with Dr. Josef Dufek to develop computational models of the chemical and microphysical processes that control the formation and transport of sulfur compounds in volcanic plumes. In conjunction with isotopic analyses of volcanic ash, these models could be used to determine the rate of entrainment in historic eruptions, a crucial factor determining the maximum height of a volcanic plume. Combining these models and historical entrainment rates with global atmospheric models, I developed tools to predict the climate forcing following a large volcanic eruption capable of delivering sulfates to the stratosphere, where they remain for long timescales and reflect sunlight, cooling the Earth's surface.

Modeling Circumstellar Debris Disks

While at CU Boulder, I worked with Dr. Meredith MacGregor to model high-resolution images of circumstellar debris disks taken by the Atacama Large Millimeter/sub-millimeter Array (ALMA). These disks form from colliding planetesimals—the remnants of planet formation—and provide valuable information about the dynamical evolution of a planetary system. I led the modeling of HD 53143, which is currently the most eccentric (non-circular) debris disk known to date. Additionally, my senior thesis measured the alignment between debris disks and their host stars, helping understand which mechanisms misalign planetary orbits with their stars' rotation axes. This work was recognized by the Department of Astrophysical and Planetary Sciences with the Theodore Snow Scholarship, awarded to the graduating student with the best honors thesis, and later published.

Detecting Exoplanets

I collaborated with Dr. David Latham and Dr. Samuel Quinn at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics and Dr. Benjamin Fulton at Caltech to search for low-mass planets around nearby stars using the radial-velocity method. This resulted in two papers identifying candidate planets around Vega, one of the brightest stars in our night sky, and Gliese 411, the fourth closest stellar system to Earth. During this time, I also maintained and added functionality to RadVel, a popular Python package used to model radial-velocity time series and search for exoplanets using Bayesian statistics.

Characterizing Sub-Stellar Objects

I spent several summers working with Dr. Michael Liu at the University of Hawaii Institute for Astronomy, fitting model atmospheres to the largest sample of benchmark ultracool dwarfs to date. This resulted in a paper characterizing systematic behavior in the model atmospheres commonly used to study ultracool dwarfs.

Outreach and Volunteering

In my current position at WIRO, I lead tours of the facility, sharing information about astronomy and engineering with the general public. I also lead several science outreach activites, including stargazing events and classes on light pollution. I previously volunteered with the Grand Mesa Observatory, located near my hometown in western Colorado. Here, I helped organize telescope visits and public talks while maintaining and operating equipment. I also developed an observational astronomy and data analysis curriculum to offer local high school students a hands-on introduction to scientific research and computer programming. While at CU Boulder, I regularly ran virtual open-house nights at the Sommers-Bausch Observatory.

Outside of astronomy, I often participate in fieldwork for the Wyoming Naturalist Program and University of Wyoming Biodiversity Institute. A short list of examples includes releasing and surveying for the endangered Wyoming Toad on the Laramie Plains, conducting amphibian surveys in Medicine Bow National Forest, and surveying for shorebirds in Bamforth National Wildlife Refuge.
